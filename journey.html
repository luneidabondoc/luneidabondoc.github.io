Chapter One · The Philippines

The Calling — Becoming a Doctor

I entered medicine with a profound sense of vocation. In the Philippines, becoming a doctor is not just a career — it is a covenant with community. I trained in a system shaped by scarcity: overcrowded wards, under-resourced hospitals, and patients who walked days to reach care. I saw, early on, that illness is rarely just biology — it is poverty, geography, and structural neglect wearing a clinical mask.

Chapter Two · The Breaking Point

Burnout & Moral Injury

The system demanded more than any one person could give. Burnout is exhaustion; moral injury is something deeper — it is the wound that forms when you are forced to act against your values, or powerless to act at all. I watched people die from preventable things. I couldn't fix what wasn't a medical problem. The gap between the care I wanted to give and the care the system allowed me to give became unbearable.

This wasn't weakness. It was my conscience working exactly as it should.

Burnout Moral Injury Reckoning
Chapter Three · The Crossing

Migration to Australia

To migrate is to grieve and hope at the same time. I left behind the people who felt like home — childhood friends, the community that knew me before I had any titles — and the social capital of being a known professional. Australia offered possibility, but also the invisibility of starting over. My qualifications, my reputation, my depth of experience — all suddenly required re-translation into a new system's language. Migration was like a second medical training: I had to learn the anatomy of a new society, its norms, its bureaucracies, its unspoken hierarchies.

Chapter Four · The Pivot

Discovering Public Health

Public health gave my moral injury a framework. Suddenly I wasn't just treating disease — I was interrogating its origins. The social determinants of health — income, housing, education, discrimination, access — became my clinical vocabulary. I found a field that matched my conviction: that fixing a person without fixing their conditions is incomplete medicine. Public health let me zoom out and act upstream.

It was also, in a way, a form of healing. My burnout had roots in powerlessness; public health gave me leverage.

Public Health Upstream Thinking Equity
Chapter Five · The Framework

Access, Equity & the Root Causes

I came to understand access to health not as a logistical problem, but as a justice problem. Why do some communities wait longer, receive less, trust less, and die sooner? Why is navigating health and welfare systems a privilege rather than a right? These questions lit a fire in me. I began to see my work not as helping individuals get services, but as dismantling the distance between people and the supports they deserve. This became my north star.

Chapter Six · The Present

NDIS Service Manager — Still Finding the Way

The National Disability Insurance Scheme is one of Australia's most ambitious equity frameworks — and also one of its most complex. As a service manager for an NDIS registered provider, I sit at the intersection of policy and personhood. Every participant I support carries their own story of marginalisation, of systems that failed them, of support that came too late. My clinical eye, my public health lens, and my immigrant's understanding of navigating bureaucracy while being underestimated — all of it is active here.

"Still finding my way" is not a deficit — it's intellectual honesty. The NDIS landscape shifts constantly. Staying curious is a form of professional integrity.

Chapter Seven · The Thread

What Has Never Changed

Across every chapter — the clinic, the migration, the pivot, the complexity — one thing has remained constant: I show up for people the system has left behind. The patient with no referral pathway. The migrant worker with no sick leave. The person with disability navigating 40 pages of planning documentation. I have always found a way to stand in that gap. That is not a job. That is who I am, expressed through vocation.

Reflection

Where I Stand

I am not a doctor who left medicine. I am a healer who expanded the definition of healing. Clinical medicine asks: how do we treat this person's illness? Public health asks: why did they get ill? Disability services asks: what does this person need to live fully? I have walked all three questions — and they are the same question, asked at different scales.

The burnout and moral injury were not failures. They were the evidence of caring too much in a system that could not absorb that care. I didn't burn out because I was weak — I burned out because the gap between my values and the system's capacity was too wide to sustain alone.

Now, in Australia, I am building that capacity. Every participant whose supports I coordinate, every barrier I help dismantle, every plan I help someone navigate — I am doing, at the service level, what public health does at the population level. I am closing the distance between people and what they need.

The way is not always clear. But my direction has never changed.
